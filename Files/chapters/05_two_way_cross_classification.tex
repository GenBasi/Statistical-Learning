\section{Two-Way Cross-Classification}

\subsection{Introduction to Two-Way Cross-Classification}

\begin{defbox}{Two-Way Cross-Classification Analysis}
A fundamental approach in Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) for examining the joint relationship, association, and dependence between two variables simultaneously.
\end{defbox}

Two-way cross-classification fits perfectly within the standard Data Science workflow:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Data Preparation:} Context comprehension, cleaning, and missing data handling.
    \item \textbf{Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA):} Finding patterns using bivariate distributions.
    \item \textbf{Confirmatory Analysis:} Statistical inference and predictive modeling.
    \item \textbf{Knowledge Extraction:} Formulating business strategies and actionable insights.
\end{enumerate}


This bidimensional analysis provides insights into:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Joint Distribution:} How observations are distributed across combinations of $X$ and $Y$.
    \item \textbf{Marginal Distributions:} The individual distributions of $X$ and $Y$ in isolation.
    \item \textbf{Conditional Relationships:} Whether knowing the value of $X$ provides predictive power over the likely value of $Y$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Cross-Classification Table (Contingency Table)}

\begin{defbox}{Two-Way Cross-Classification Table}
A bidimensional frequency distribution (often called a Contingency Table) that cross-classifies statistical units based on the distinct modalities of a row variable $X$ ($i=1\dots I$) and a column variable $Y$ ($j=1\dots J$).
\end{defbox}

\textbf{Mathematical Structure:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $n_{ij}$: \textbf{Joint frequency} (the count of observations where $X=x_i$ AND $Y=y_j$).
    \item $n_{i+}$: \textbf{Row margin} (the total frequency of $X=x_i$, regardless of $Y$). Calculated as $\sum_{j=1}^{J} n_{ij}$.
    \item $n_{+j}$: \textbf{Column margin} (the total frequency of $Y=y_j$, regardless of $X$). Calculated as $\sum_{i=1}^{I} n_{ij}$.
    \item $n$: \textbf{Total observations} ($n = \sum n_{i+} = \sum n_{+j}$).
\end{itemize}

%==============================================================================
\subsubsection{Marginal and Conditional (Partial) Distributions}

A single contingency table contains multiple distinct distributions. Understanding the difference between them is the foundation of Conditional Probability in Machine Learning:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Marginal Distributions:} The univariate distribution of one variable, obtained by summing across the other variable.
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Marginal of $X$: The row totals ($n_{i+}$).
        \item Marginal of $Y$: The column totals ($n_{+j}$).
    \end{itemize}
    
    \vspace{0.15cm}
    \item \textbf{Conditional (Partial) Distributions:} The distribution of one variable \textit{given} that the other variable is fixed at a specific modality.
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $Y | X = x_i$: How $Y$ is distributed strictly within row $i$ (using proportions $\frac{n_{ij}}{n_{i+}}$).
        \item $X | Y = y_j$: How $X$ is distributed strictly within column $j$ (using proportions $\frac{n_{ij}}{n_{+j}}$).
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[H] 
    \centering\includegraphics[scale=0.9]{51.Marginal and Partial Frequency Distribution.jpg}
    \caption{Marginal and Partial Frequency Distribution}\captionsetup{font=footnotesize}
\end{figure}

Thus, a table with $I$ rows and $J$ columns actually contains $1$ joint distribution, $2$ marginal distributions, and $I+J$ conditional distributions!

%==============================================================================
\vspace{0.3cm}
\begin{example}{\textbf{Marginal and Conditional Distributions (Gender vs. Glasses)}}

Let $X$ be Gender (Male/Female) and $Y$ be Glasses Usage (No/Yes) for a sample of 100 people.

\vspace{0.2cm}
\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
\begin{tabular}{|c|cc|c|}
\hline
\textbf{Gender $\backslash$ Glasses} & \textbf{NO ($y_1$)} & \textbf{YES ($y_2$)} & \textbf{Total (Marginal $X$)} \\
\hline
\textbf{Male ($x_1$)} & 20 & 40 & \textbf{60} \\
\textbf{Female ($x_2$)} & 20 & 20 & \textbf{40} \\
\hline
\textbf{Total (Marginal $Y$)} & \textbf{40} & \textbf{60} & \textbf{$n=100$} \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\vspace{0.2cm}

\textbf{1. Joint Distribution ($X \cap Y$):}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $n_{12} = 40$: There are 40 males who use glasses.
\end{itemize}

\pagebreak
\textbf{2. Marginal Distributions:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{Marginal $X$:} 60\% of the sample is Male ($60/100$), 40\% is Female.
    \item \textit{Marginal $Y$:} 40\% don't use glasses, 60\% use glasses ($60/100$).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{3. Conditional Distributions ($Y|X$):}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $P(Y=\text{YES} | X=\text{Male}) = \frac{40}{60} = 66.7\%$
    \item $P(Y=\text{YES} | X=\text{Female}) = \frac{20}{40} = 50.0\%$
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Data Science Insight:} 
By comparing the conditional distributions, we see that Males have a higher rate of glasses usage than Females (66.7\% vs 50.0\%). Because these conditional distributions differ from the marginal distribution (which is 60\% overall), we can conclude that Gender and Glasses Usage are \textbf{not independent}. Knowing a person's gender gives us predictive power about their likelihood of wearing glasses!
\end{example}

\subsection{Types of Two-Way Cross-Classification Data Analysis}

Different analytical approaches are required depending on the measurement scale of the variables involved. The three main types of analysis form a strict hierarchy, moving from the most general (categorical) to the most specific (linear).

%==============================================================================
\subsubsection{1. Categorical Data Analysis (Contingency Tables)}

\begin{defbox}{Categorical Data Analysis}
Appropriate when both variables are nominal or ordinal. The primary goal is to analyze the \textbf{deviation from independence in distribution} to see if the distribution of one variable changes across the categories of the other.
\end{defbox}

\textbf{Sub-approaches:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Association (Symmetric):} Measures mutual association without assuming a cause-and-effect relationship (e.g., analyzing if two fault codes tend to appear together).
    \item \textbf{Predictability (Dependence):} Treats one variable as the predictor and the other as the response (e.g., predicting a pass/fail quality check based on the assembly line shift).
\end{itemize}
\textit{Common Test:} Chi-Square test for independence.

%==============================================================================
\subsubsection{2. Correlation Analysis (Mean Dependence)}

\begin{defbox}{Correlation Analysis}
Appropriate when analyzing the relationship between a \textbf{categorical stratifying factor} (independent variable) and a \textbf{numerical target} (dependent variable). The goal is to analyze the \textbf{deviation from independence in mean}.
\end{defbox}

This analysis measures whether knowing the categorical group provides information about the expected value (mean) of the numerical variable.

\vs
 \textit{Common Test:} ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) and Eta-squared ($\eta^2$).

%==============================================================================
\subsubsection{3. Linear Correlation Analysis}

\begin{defbox}{Linear Correlation Analysis}
Appropriate when \textbf{both variables are continuous numerical data}. The goal is to determine the \textbf{strength and direction of a linear relationship}.
\end{defbox}

This is the most restrictive form of analysis. It assumes the relationship can be modeled by a straight line, ranging from $-1$ (perfect inverse correlation) to $+1$ (perfect positive correlation), with $0$ indicating no linear correlation.
\newline \textit{Common Test:} Pearson Correlation Coefficient ($r$), Linear Regression.

%==============================================================================
\subsubsection{The Analytical Hierarchy}

These three approaches form a nested hierarchy of statistical power:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Categorical (Most General):} Makes the fewest assumptions. Can be applied to numerical data if you bin it into categories (e.g., converting continuous age into "Young/Old"), but you lose information.
    \item \textbf{Correlation (Specific):} Requires at least one numerical variable.
    \item \textbf{Linear Correlation (Most Specific):} Requires both variables to be numerical and assumes a linear relationship.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Data Science Rule of Thumb:} Always use the most specific analysis your variable types allow, as it provides the highest statistical power. However, if assumptions are violated (e.g., a numerical relationship is heavily non-linear), step back to a more general approach.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\begin{example}{\textbf{Choosing the Right Analysis: Powertrain Data Science}}

Imagine you are analyzing telemetry and calibration data from a fleet of test vehicles. You have different variables and need to choose the correct cross-classification analysis:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Scenario A (Categorical Data Analysis):} 
    \newline \textit{Variables:} Driving Mode (Eco, Sport, Snow) vs. OBD Fault Category (Emissions, Transmission, Ignition).
    \newline \textit{Why:} Both are categorical. You want to know if the distribution of fault types is independent of the driving mode selected by the user.

    \vs
    \item \textbf{Scenario B (Correlation Analysis):}
    \newline \textit{Variables:} Engine Calibration Map (Map 1, Map 2, Map 3) vs. Average Fuel Consumption (L/100km).
    \newline \textit{Why:} One categorical factor, one numerical target. You want to see if the \textit{mean} fuel consumption significantly changes depending on which calibration map is flashed to the ECU.

    \vs
    \item \textbf{Scenario C (Linear Correlation Analysis):}
    \newline \textit{Variables:} Engine RPM (continuous) vs. Coolant Temperature (continuous).
    \newline \textit{Why:} Both are numerical. You want to measure if an increase in RPM directly and linearly correlates with an increase in operating temperature.
\end{itemize}
\end{example}

\subsection{Contingency Table Analysis: Independence in Distribution}

\textbf{Profile analysis} provides a geometric interpretation of cross-classification tables by treating rows and columns as points in multidimensional space, facilitating the visualization and understanding of relationships between categories.

%==============================================================================
\subsubsection*{Profiles and Centroids}

In Contingency Table analysis, we examine how categories distribute across different conditions by using \textbf{profiles} and \textbf{centroids}.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Profiles} provide a normalized view of the table by converting absolute frequencies into conditional distributions ($f_{ij} = n_{ij}/n$). This allows fair comparisons across tables of different sizes.
    
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Row Profiles} describe how the column variable $Y$ is distributed within each category of the row variable $X$. The $i$-th row profile is the conditional distribution of $Y$ given $X = x_i$:
        \begin{equation}
            \text{Row Profile}_i = \left(\frac{f_{i1}}{f_{i+}}, \ldots, \frac{f_{ij}}{f_{i+}}, \ldots, \frac{f_{iJ}}{f_{i+}}\right)
        \end{equation}
        where $f_{i+}$ is the marginal relative frequency for row $i$. The elements sum to $1$.
        
        \item \textbf{Column Profiles} describe how the row variable $X$ is distributed within each category of the column variable $Y$. The $j$-th column profile is:
        \begin{equation}
            \text{Column Profile}_j = \left(\frac{f_{1j}}{f_{+j}}, \ldots, \frac{f_{ij}}{f_{+j}}, \ldots, \frac{f_{Ij}}{f_{+j}}\right)
        \end{equation}
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Centroids} represent the "average profile" of all rows or all columns, acting as the \textbf{center of gravity} of the dataset.
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Centroid of all row profiles:} A weighted average of row profiles, which mathematically results exactly in the \textbf{marginal distribution of $Y$}: $(f_{+1}, \ldots, f_{+J})$.
        \item \textbf{Centroid of all column profiles:} Results exactly in the \textbf{marginal distribution of $X$}: $(f_{1+}, \ldots, f_{I+})$.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Data Science Intuition:} The centroid serves as a benchmark. Profiles that sit far from the centroid in geometric space indicate categories with highly unusual conditional behaviors. Profiles close to the centroid represent "average" or expected behavior.

    \centering\includegraphics[scale=0.6]{50.Contigency Table.jpg}
    \caption{Contingency Table Analysis}\captionsetup{font=footnotesize}
\end{figure}

%==============================================================================
\vspace{0.3cm}
\begin{example}{Computing Profiles and Centroids}
Let's analyze the Gender $\times$ Glasses Usage dataset ($n=100$).

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
\begin{tabular}{|c|cc|c|}
\hline
\textbf{Gender $\backslash$ Glasses} & \textbf{NO} & \textbf{YES} & \textbf{Total (Rel. Freq.)} \\
\hline
\textbf{Male} & 0.20 & 0.40 & 0.60 \\
\textbf{Female} & 0.20 & 0.20 & 0.40 \\
\hline
\textbf{Total} & 0.40 & 0.60 & 1.00 \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Row Profiles:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Male Profile = $(\frac{0.20}{0.60}, \frac{0.40}{0.60}) = (0.333, 0.667)$
        \item Female Profile = $(\frac{0.20}{0.40}, \frac{0.20}{0.40}) = (0.500, 0.500)$
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Row Centroid:} The marginal distribution of Glasses = $(0.40, 0.60)$.
    
    \item \textbf{Geometric Distance (Deviation from Average):} We can calculate the Euclidean distance of each profile from the centroid to see who deviates the most from the "average human":
    $$d_{\text{Male}} = \sqrt{(0.333-0.40)^2 + (0.667-0.60)^2} = 0.095$$
    $$d_{\text{Female}} = \sqrt{(0.50-0.40)^2 + (0.50-0.60)^2} = 0.141$$
    The Female profile deviates more heavily from the average dataset behavior.
\end{itemize}
\end{example}

%==============================================================================
\subsubsection*{Independence in Distribution}

\begin{defbox}{Independence in Distribution}
Two variables $X$ and $Y$ are independent if and only if their joint distribution is exactly the product of their marginal distributions:
\begin{equation}
    f_{ij} = f_{i+} \cdot f_{+j} \quad \text{for all } i, j
\end{equation}
\end{defbox}

If variables are perfectly independent, \textbf{all row profiles will be perfectly identical to each other} (and thus identical to the centroid). Knowing the value of $X$ provides absolutely zero predictive power over $Y$.

%==============================================================================
\subsubsection{Association: Symmetric Interdependence Analysis}

When variables are NOT independent, we must quantify the strength of their relationship.

\begin{defbox}{Pearson's Mean Square Contingency Coefficient ($\phi^2$)}
A symmetric measure of association for categorical data. It represents the "inertia" of the profiles—how much they spread out from the centroid.
\begin{equation}
    \phi^2 = \frac{\chi^2}{n} = \sum_{i=1}^{I} \sum_{j=1}^{J} \frac{(f_{ij} - f_{i+}f_{+j})^2}{f_{i+}f_{+j}}
\end{equation}
where $\chi^2$ is the standard Chi-Square statistic.
\end{defbox}

\textbf{Properties \& Interpretation of $\phi$ ($\sqrt{\phi^2}$):}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\phi^2 = 0$ implies perfect independence.
    \item For a $2 \times 2$ table, $\phi$ ranges from $0$ to $1$. In Machine Learning, for binary classification matrices, $\phi$ is mathematically identical to the \textbf{Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC)}.
    \item $|\phi| < 0.1$ (Negligible), $0.1 - 0.3$ (Weak), $0.3 - 0.5$ (Moderate), $> 0.5$ (Strong).
\end{itemize}

%==============================================================================
\subsubsection{Predictability: Non-Symmetric Dependence Analysis}

In predictive modeling, variables play asymmetric roles (Predictor $X \rightarrow$ Target $Y$). We need an index to measure how well $X$ predicts $Y$.

\pagebreak
\begin{defbox}{Goodman and Kruskal $\tau$ Index}
The non-symmetric version of Pearson's coefficient. It quantifies the proportional reduction in prediction error when using the predictor variable $X$ compared to ignoring it.
\begin{equation}
    \tau_{Y|X} = \frac{\sum_i \sum_j \frac{f_{ij}^2}{f_{i+}} - \sum_j f_{+j}^2}{1 - \sum_j f_{+j}^2}
\end{equation}
The denominator is the \textbf{Gini Heterogeneity Index ($H_{\text{Gini}}$)} of the Target $Y$, representing the baseline unpredictability.
\end{defbox}

\textbf{Properties:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\tau_{Y|X} = 0$: $X$ provides zero predictive power (Independence).
    \item $\tau_{Y|X} = 1$: $X$ perfectly predicts $Y$ (Deterministic mapping).
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.2cm}
\begin{example}{\textbf{Understanding $\tau$ and Gini Heterogeneity}}

Assume we have a Predictor (Group A, B, C) and a Target.

\textbf{Scenario 1: $\tau=0$ (Zero Predictability)}
The Target distribution is identical across all Groups (e.g., 33\% Yes, 67\% No). Knowing the Group tells you nothing new. $\tau_{Y|X} = 0$.

\textbf{Scenario 2: $\tau=1$ (Perfect Predictability, 2 Target Categories)}
Group A $\rightarrow$ NO. Group B $\rightarrow$ YES. Group C $\rightarrow$ NO.
There is no ambiguity. If you know the Group, you know the Target with 100\% certainty. 
Base Heterogeneity $H_{\text{Gini}} = 0.445$. $\tau = 1$.

\textbf{Scenario 3: $\tau=1$ (Perfect Predictability, 3 Target Categories)}
Group A $\rightarrow$ LOW. Group B $\rightarrow$ MEDIUM. Group C $\rightarrow$ HIGH.
Again, 100\% certainty. However, because the Target has 3 categories instead of 2, the baseline unpredictability of the dataset is naturally higher ($H_{\text{Gini}} = 0.653$). 

While both Scenario 2 and 3 achieve $\tau=1$, Scenario 3 is "eliminating" a much larger amount of baseline heterogeneity, capturing a more complex 3x3 mapping.
\end{example}

\subsection{Correlation Analysis: Independence in Mean}

When one variable is \textbf{categorical} (the stratifying variable, $X$) and the other is \textbf{numerical} (the target variable, $Y$), we can test whether the expected value (mean) of the target changes across different categories.

\begin{defbox}{Independence in Mean}
Independence in mean holds when the conditional mean of the target variable for any category of the stratifying variable is equal to the overall general mean:
\begin{equation}
    \mu_{Y|X=x_i} = \mu_Y \quad \text{for any row } i = 1, \ldots, I
\end{equation}
where $\mu_{Y|X=x_i}$ is the conditional mean of $Y$ given $X = x_i$, and $\mu_Y$ is the overall mean of $Y$.
\end{defbox}

\textbf{Practical Meaning:} If Independence in Mean holds, changing the category of $X$ has zero effect on the average outcome of $Y$. 

\vspace{0.25cm}
\begin{example}{\textbf{Independence in Mean: Salary by Gender}}

Suppose we analyze the salary ($Y$, in thousands) of 120 employees stratified by Gender ($X$). 
After computing the conditional means from the frequency table, we find:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mu_{Y|\text{Male}} = 56.0\text{k}$
    \item $\mu_{Y|\text{Female}} = 54.3\text{k}$
    \item $\mu_Y \text{ (Overall)} = 55.0\text{k}$
\end{itemize}
Since $\mu_{Y|\text{Male}} \neq \mu_{Y|\text{Female}} \neq \mu_Y$, there is \textbf{no independence in mean}. Knowing the gender provides information about the expected salary (revealing a pay gap of \$1.7k in this sample).
\end{example}

%==============================================================================

\subsubsection{Pearson's Correlation Ratio ($\eta^2$)}

To quantify \textit{how much} the categorical variable impacts the numerical target, we look at the variance.



\begin{defbox}{Pearson's Correlation Ratio}
A non-symmetric measure that calculates the proportion of total variance in the target variable $Y$ that is explained by the categorical groups $X$. 

The total deviance (variance) of $Y$ is decomposed into Between-Group Deviation ($Dev(B)$) and Within-Group Deviation ($Dev(W)$):
\begin{equation}
    Dev(Y) = Dev(B) + Dev(W)
\end{equation}
The correlation ratio $\eta^2$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    \eta_{Y|X}^2 = \frac{Dev(B)}{Dev(Y)} = 1 - \frac{Dev(W)}{Dev(Y)}
\end{equation}
\end{defbox}

\textbf{Properties of $\eta^2$:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $0 \leq \eta_{Y|X}^2 \leq 1$.
    \item $\eta_{Y|X}^2 = 0$: No relationship (all group means are exactly equal to the overall mean).
    \item $\eta_{Y|X}^2 = 1$: Perfect relationship (zero variance within groups; every member of a group has the exact same value).
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.2cm}
\begin{example}{\textbf{Correlation Ratio: Shelf Position vs. Sales}}

You are testing if placing a product on the Top, Middle, or Bottom shelf affects daily sales. 
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Group Means:} Top ($5.75$), Middle ($4.00$), Bottom ($5.50$). Overall Mean: $5.08$.
    \item \textbf{Variance Decomposition:} By calculating the squared errors, we find $Dev(B) = 0.597$ and the Total Deviance $Dev(Y) = 6.743$.
    \item \textbf{Result:} $\eta_{Y|X}^2 = \frac{0.597}{6.743} \approx 0.0886$ (or $8.86\%$).
\end{itemize}
\textbf{Data Science Insight:} Only $\sim8.9\%$ of the variance in sales is explained by the shelf position. The remaining $91.1\%$ is driven by other factors (price, brand, seasonality). The relationship exists, but it is extremely weak.
\end{example}

\subsection{Linear Correlation Analysis}

When \textbf{both} variables are numerical, we can measure if they move together along a straight line.



\begin{defbox}{Bravais-Pearson Linear Correlation Coefficient ($\rho$)}
A symmetric metric that measures the strength and direction of the linear relationship between two continuous numerical variables, $X$ and $Y$. It is calculated as the covariance divided by the product of the standard deviations:
\begin{equation}
    \rho_{XY} = \frac{\sigma_{XY}}{\sigma_X \sigma_Y}
\end{equation}
where $-1 \leq \rho_{XY} \leq +1$.
\end{defbox}

\textbf{Interpretation:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\rho_{XY} = +1$: Perfect positive linear relationship (points fall exactly on an upward straight line).
    \item $\rho_{XY} = -1$: Perfect negative linear relationship (points fall exactly on a downward straight line).
    \item $\rho_{XY} = 0$: \textbf{No linear relationship} (points form a random cloud, or a perfect non-linear shape).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Geometric Interpretation:} 
If you standardize both variables into Z-scores ($z_x, z_y$), $\rho$ is simply the average of their products: $\rho_{XY} = \frac{1}{n} \sum z_x z_y$. Points in the 1st and 3rd quadrants increase correlation, while points in the 2nd and 4th quadrants decrease it.

%==============================================================================
\vspace{0.3cm}
\subsection{The Hierarchy of Independence (Crucial for Machine Learning)}

Understanding how these metrics connect is one of the most important theoretical foundations in statistics and feature selection. 

There is a strict mathematical relationship between the linear correlation coefficient and the correlation ratio:
\begin{equation}
    0 \leq \rho_{XY}^2 \leq \eta_{Y|X}^2 \leq 1
\end{equation}
This proves that \textit{linear correlation ($\rho^2$) is just a subset of general dependence ($\eta^2$)}.

This leads to the \textbf{Logical Hierarchy of Independence}:
\begin{equation*}
    \text{Independence in Distribution} \Rightarrow \text{Independence in Mean} \Rightarrow \text{No Linear Correlation } (\rho=0)
\end{equation*}

\textbf{The Rules of Inference:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Top-Down Guarantee:} If two variables are perfectly independent in their distribution, they will automatically have independent means, and their linear correlation will be exactly zero.
    \item \textbf{Bottom-Up Fallacy (The Trap):} The reverse is \textbf{FALSE}. If $\rho = 0$, it \textbf{DOES NOT} mean the variables are independent. 
\end{enumerate}



\textbf{Data Science Application:}
Imagine a scatter plot where points form a perfect "U" shape (a parabola, $Y = X^2$). 
If you blindly run a Pearson correlation test, it will return $\rho = 0$, leading a junior analyst to drop the feature assuming it has no relationship with the target. However, the variables are perfectly dependent ($\eta^2 = 1$). You just need a non-linear model (like a Decision Tree) or a feature transformation to exploit it.
